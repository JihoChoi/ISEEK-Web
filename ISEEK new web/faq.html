F.A.Q.

You have a question? Someone else might have had the exact same one. Let's find out!

What is ISEEK?

ISEEK stands for the Institute for Social Empowerment through Entrepreneurship and Knowledge. Our primary goal is to empower the under-privileged and poverty-stricken young people in the world to lift themselves out of poverty and lead healthy and prosperous lives. We achieve our goal through innovative, technology-enabled approaches to entrepreneurship and self-care education, on-the-job training, and ICT support for the target demographic to start and sustain businesses and manage their health.

What is Saksham?

Saksham is one of ISEEK’s ongoing programs. Its first project is based in the state of Jharkhand in India, where a large segment of the population lives below the subsistence level. Project Saksham will serve as a brand and a channel through which those associated with ISEEK will be able to sell and market the products they make - supporting their craft and providing them with continual business.

Who implements the projects?

In collaboration with local governments, NGOs, and universities, ISEEK will deliver skill development programs in novel ways First, Project Saksham operates through a group-based entrepreneurship model, in which Self-Help Groups are created and then formed into small businesses. Second, ISEEK provides a holistic technology platform that allows for scale and intrinsic monitoring and evaluation that has yet to be created in the development sector. We do not perform charity, we simply provide resources for small businesses and social entrepreneurs to establish a livelihood.

What is an SHG?

SHG stands for Self-Help Group. An SHG is a village-based financial intermediary committee usually composed of 10–20 local women or men. Each SHG working with ISEEK will act as one business with supervision from partner NGOs who will help act as liaisons between ISEEK and the SHGs.

What are SILCs?

Savings and Internal Lending Communities (SILC) is a holistic programming approach that offers households a strategy to protect assets, smooth cash flow, and increase income. In comparison to traditional microfinance institutions that face limitations in servicing the financial needs of vulnerable groups such as women, poor farmers, orphans and youth, SILC is able to provide flexible financial solutions to these marginalized groups in a sustainable manner. The program has been quite successful in Africa and ISEEK intends to bring this program to other parts of the world such as the state of Jharkhand in India. Currently, the program in Jharkhand is being sponsored by Central Government of India, and thus the smartphones and internet access in India will be provided by them. In other countries, ISEEK will partner with telecommunication companies or participating in programs sponsored by such companies to help underprivileged population. http://edu.care.org/Documents/Savings%20and%20Internal%20Lending%20Communities%20in%20Africa.pdf

How do NGOs get selected to be a part of the program?

ISEEK partners with organizations that are based locally in the community in which we operate. The reason we do this is because local NGOs have an expertise in a particular community, thus making it easier for them to support one particular SHG in the community.

How do small business groups get selected to be a part of the program?

The target population in each of ISEEK’s implementation countries depends on the context and the primary causes of poverty in the region. In India’s state of Jharkhand, for example, ISEEK is targeting the 550,000 women between the ages of 16 and 25 who live under the subsistence level and are either illiterate or have dropped out of school before completing the 4th grade. In Bulgaria, ISEEK is targeting the Roma population, a marginalized community where the average age of marriage is 15. Alleviating poverty from this target group requires a clear road to empowerment, the deployment of a sustainable, customizable, repeatable and scalable information and communication technology solution, and an intervention model that translates gained knowledge into practical applications.

Why collective entrepreneurship instead of individual entrepreneurship?

Collective entrepreneurship is a unique aspect of the ISEEK model that presents a fascinating social case, one that can be iteratively analyzed and improved upon. The challenge in implementing a collective entrepreneurship model is to avoid free riders. We believe that through the transparency of the technology platform, however, business members will hold each other accountable.
